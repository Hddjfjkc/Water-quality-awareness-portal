What Lurks in Your Water

Common pollutants that affect drinking water quality, their health impacts, and acceptable concentration limits.

● High Risk
🔩

Lead (Pb)

Leaches from old pipes and fixtures. Causes severe neurological damage in children; no safe level exists for developing brains.

WHO Limit: 0.01 mg/LDanger Level: 0.05+ mg/L
● High Risk
☣️

Arsenic (As)

Naturally occurring in groundwater. Long-term exposure linked to cancer, cardiovascular disease, and diabetes.

WHO Limit: 0.01 mg/LNatural BG: 0.001 mg/L
● Medium Risk
💊

Nitrates (NO₃⁻)

Agricultural runoff and sewage. Causes methemoglobinemia ("blue baby syndrome") in infants under 6 months.

WHO Limit: 50 mg/LRisk: 90+ mg/L
● High Risk
🧪

PFAS / Forever Chemicals

Industrial chemicals that accumulate in blood. Associated with cancer, hormone disruption, and immune system impairment.

EPA Limit: 4 pptDetected: 70+ ppt common
● Medium Risk
🦠

E. coli & Bacteria

Fecal contamination indicator. Causes gastrointestinal illness, kidney failure, and can be fatal for immunocompromised individuals.

Safe Limit: 0 CFU/100mLRisk: any detection
● Lower Risk
🌫️

Chlorine Byproducts

Disinfection byproducts (THMs, HAAs) form when chlorine reacts with organic matter. Long-term exposure may increase cancer risk.

EPA Limit: 0.08 mg/LCommon: 0.02 mg/L
● High Risk
⚡

Mercury (Hg)

Industrial discharge and coal burning. Accumulates in fish. Causes neurological damage, kidney failure, and reproductive harm.

WHO Limit: 0.006 mg/LDanger: 0.01+ mg/L
● Medium Risk
🔬

Microplastics

Fragments from plastic pollution detected in 94% of U.S. tap water. Potential endocrine disruption; research ongoing.

No official limit yetResearch ongoing

Key Milestones in Water Quality History

1854

The Broad Street Cholera Outbreak

Dr. John Snow identified a contaminated water pump as the source of a cholera epidemic in London, founding modern epidemiology and linking water quality to public health.

1914

First U.S. Drinking Water Standard

The U.S. Public Health Service set the first federal bacteriological standard for drinking water — limited to water supplies used in interstate commerce.

1972

Clean Water Act (U.S.)

Established the basic structure for regulating pollutant discharges into the waters of the United States, transforming water quality regulation.

2010

UN Recognizes Water as Human Right

The United Nations General Assembly explicitly recognized the human right to water and sanitation, acknowledging clean water is essential for the full enjoyment of life.

2015

UN Sustainable Development Goal 6

SDG 6 sets global targets to ensure availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all by 2030.

2024

EPA PFAS Maximum Contaminant Levels

The U.S. EPA finalized the first-ever national drinking water standard for PFAS "forever chemicals," setting maximum contaminant levels as low as 4 parts per trillion.

Take Action

Water Conservation Tips

Simple, effective actions individuals and communities can take to protect and conserve water quality.

01
🚰

Test Your Tap Water

Use certified home testing kits or contact your local utility for Consumer Confidence Reports. Know what's actually in your water before treating it.

02
🌱

Reduce Chemical Runoff

Choose native plants that require less fertilizer. Use organic alternatives and apply pesticides sparingly — excess chemicals leach into groundwater.

03
♻️

Dispose of Hazardous Waste Properly

Never pour medications, motor oil, or household chemicals down drains. Use designated collection programs — these substances contaminate treatment systems.

04
🪣

Fix Leaks & Conserve

A dripping faucet wastes up to 20 gallons per day. Fixing household leaks can save ~10% on your water bill and reduces pressure on water infrastructure.

05
🏗️

Support Green Infrastructure

Advocate for permeable pavements, rain gardens, and green roofs in your community — these reduce storm water runoff that overwhelms treatment plants.

06
📢

Stay Informed & Advocate

Attend public meetings, comment on permits, and vote on water-related policies. Water quality is a public health issue that requires community engagement.
